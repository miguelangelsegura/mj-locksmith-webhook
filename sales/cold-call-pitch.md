# Cold-Call Pitch — Locksmith AI Receptionist (v1)

**Owner:** Abdul · **Use:** phone outreach to locksmith owners · **Status:** v1 draft for Friday

This script is built from proven, well-regarded sales methodologies, adapted to our product
and the locksmith reality. Read the structure, then make it your own — say it like a human,
not a script.

**Frameworks it draws on**
- *Fanatical Prospecting* (Jeb Blount) — the permission-based opener / "ledge," and the rule
  that the goal of the call is the **next step (a demo), not the sale**.
- *SPIN Selling* (Neil Rackham) — Situation → **Problem → Implication** → Need-payoff
  questioning; you sell by making the cost of the problem real, not by listing features.
- *The Challenger Sale* (Dixon & Adamson) — lead with a **teaching insight** that reframes
  how they see their missed calls; take control of the conversation.
- *Never Split the Difference* (Chris Voss) — disarm with honesty, use **calibrated
  questions** and a **"no"-oriented** ask to lower pressure.

**The one number that sells this:** ~**two-thirds of locksmith calls come in evenings,
nights, weekends, and holidays** — exactly when you can't pick up — and a locked-out customer
just dials the next locksmith. A mid-size shop gets ~40 calls/week and misses ~9.
*(Sources at bottom.)*

## The call (≈60–90 seconds to the ask)

### 1. Opener — get permission, disarm (Blount "ledge" + Voss honesty)
> "Hey [Name], it's Abdul — I'll be straight with you, this is a cold call. Give me 30 seconds
> to tell you why I called, and if it's not useful you can tell me to get lost. Fair enough?"

*Why:* naming the cold call disarms; asking permission lowers defenses and earns the 30
seconds. A "fair enough?" invites a small yes.

### 2. The teaching insight — reframe the problem (Challenger + SPIN Problem/Implication)
> "Most locksmith owners I talk to think they're catching their calls. But about two-thirds of
> locksmith calls come in after hours or when you're already on a job — and a customer locked
> out at 9pm doesn't leave a voicemail, they just call the next guy on Google. So the calls
> you miss don't show up anywhere — they're just lost jobs you never knew you had. For a shop
> your size that's often six to nine jobs a week."

*Why:* this teaches them something about their own business and quantifies the pain. Don't
pitch the product yet.

### 3. Discovery — make it theirs (SPIN questions; shut up and listen)
> "Quick question — when you're under a car or it's the middle of the night and the phone
> rings, what happens to that call right now?"
>
> "And roughly, how many calls a week do you figure slip past you?"

*Why:* their own answer is more persuasive than your claim. Let them say the number.

### 4. The solution — short, concrete (tie to what they just said)
> "Here's what we do. We give you an AI receptionist that answers in your shop's name, 24/7 —
> sounds like a real person, gets the lockout type, the address, and a callback number, and
> texts you the full job the second the call ends, so you ring back a warm lead. You keep your
> own number, your customers dial the same number they always have. Nothing changes on your
> end except you stop losing the after-hours jobs."

### 5. The ask — low-pressure next step (Blount: sell the demo; Voss: no-oriented)
> "Would you be opposed to me showing you exactly how it'd sound for [Shop Name] — 15 minutes
> this week? Or honestly, easier: call our demo line right now, [DEMO NUMBER], talk to it like
> you're a locked-out customer, and if it's not impressive you can hang up on me."

*Why:* "Would you be opposed" lets them say *no* to feel in control while moving forward; the
live demo is our strongest asset — let them experience it.

## Objection handling

- **"How much is it?"**
  > "Fair question — it's [PRICE PLACEHOLDER — fill after pricing is set], less than one job a
  > month. But before price, let me show you it actually works for your shop — if it doesn't
  > catch you real leads, price doesn't matter. Got 15 minutes this week?"
  *Anchor to value (one recovered job pays for it), then redirect to the demo.*

- **"I already have voicemail / an answering service."**
  > "Right — and how often does a locked-out person actually leave a voicemail? Almost never;
  > they call the next locksmith. An answering service takes a message too — this books the
  > lead and texts it to you in real time, in your name, for a fraction of what a service costs."

- **"AI sounds robotic / customers will hate it."**
  > "I'd think the same — that's exactly why I want you to call the demo line yourself right
  > now and judge it. If it sounds like a robot, I've wasted your time and you hang up."

- **"I'm too busy for this."**
  > "That's the whole point — you're too busy to answer every call, which means you're losing
  > the ones you can't get to. This is the thing that catches them while you're busy. Two
  > minutes on a demo call and you'll see it."

- **"Send me some info."**
  > "Happy to — but the info is the demo; reading a PDF won't tell you how it sounds. Tell you
  > what: call the demo line, and I'll text you a one-pager right after. What number's best?"
  *(Capture the cell + permission to text → that's a small commitment.)*

## Why us (keep in your back pocket — differentiators vs the big players)
- **Local, Canadian, owner-run** — you talk to us, not a ticket queue; CASL-compliant.
- **Keep your number** — call forwarding, no porting, no risk.
- **It's the after-hours money** — we're not replacing your day; we're catching the nights and
  weekends you're already losing.
- **Live in [X] minutes, no long contract** — try it, keep it only if it pays.

## Reminders
- The goal of the call is **the demo, not the sale.** Every road leads to "call the demo line"
  or "15 minutes this week."
- Talk less than half the time. The discovery questions do the selling.
